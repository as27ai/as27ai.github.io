Who Captures the Profits in the GenAI Stack?

GenAI isn’t just another innovation—it’s the foundation of the next wave of tools, business models, and user experiences. This piece maps the stack, explains why compute dominates costs today, and outlines where profits accrue now and next. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

1) The GenAI stack today

A four-layer view—Silicon/AI accelerators, Infrastructure/Cloud, Platform & Models, and Applications—best explains how capability turns into value. Most of the ongoing cost of an LLM API call sits in compute (GPUs/TPUs), not software OPEX. This puts outsized economics at the silicon layer. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

2) How major players position across the stack

  • Google: vertically integrated (TPU → GCP → Gemini → Workspace).
  • Meta: consumer reach + open source (Llama, MTIA chips).
  • AWS: infra-first with custom silicon (Trainium) to reduce NVIDIA dependency.
  • Microsoft: breadth across stack (Azure, OpenAI partnership, Copilot).
  • NVIDIA: “picks & shovels” provider dominating accelerators & CUDA ecosystem. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

3) Why NVIDIA captures the most value (for now)

With >80% share in AI training/deployment GPUs, leadership in TOP500 systems, and CUDA lock-in, NVIDIA monetizes the scarcest, least-substitutable layer. High ASPs and demand for H100/B200/GB200 sustain margins even as models and apps compete on price and features. Risks: model efficiency, custom silicon, capex cycles, and export controls. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

4) Blurring boundaries across layers

Unlike the PC/Cloud eras, GenAI players actively cross layers—chipmakers launch cloud services, clouds launch models, and model labs ship apps. The motives: margin protection, user lock-in, speed, and ecosystem control (e.g., CUDA, agent protocols). :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

5) How winners will differentiate

  • Proprietary assets: specialized hardware, data, and software moats.
  • Integration depth: embed into daily workflows to raise switching costs (e.g., Copilot suite).
  • Adaptive expansion: move quickly as stack boundaries shift. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
